Rhetoric: The Art of Strategic Communication

Master the voice that commands and persuades.

Precision. Pressure. Mastery.

Course Overview

At X Veritas Collegiate, Rhetoric is not about flowery language — it is strategic communication under fire. This course trains students to influence, challenge, and command through speech and writing. You will learn to craft arguments with impact, dismantle opposing views ethically, and speak with authority in any room.

Whether delivering a 60-second argument in a crisis simulation or composing a high-stakes brief, students will master rhetorical discipline.

What You’ll Learn

  • The Classical Rhetorical Triangle: Ethos (credibility), Logos (logic), Pathos (emotion)
  • Structure of a persuasive argument
  • The five canons of rhetoric: Invention, Arrangement, Style, Memory, Delivery
  • Discourse ethics & integrity under pressure
  • Counterargument anticipation and response
  • Rapid-response oratory & debate
  • Strategic speechwriting & briefing

Core Materials

Primary Readings:

  • Aristotle’s Rhetoric (MIT Classics) — Foundational text
  • Thank You for Arguing by Jay Heinrichs — Modern tactics, accessible style

Recommended Videos:

  • Crash Course: What is Rhetoric
  • Critical Thinking & Arguing

Evaluation Criteria

  • Weekly speech labs (3–5 minute presentations)
  • Strategic briefing drills (individual and paired)
  • Logical deconstruction of political/media speeches
  • Op-Ed writing challenge
  • Final: 5-minute persuasive speech before public panel

Rhetorical Situations You Will Face

Situation Objective

Ethical Debate Respond under pressure, stay composed
Campaign Proposal Evoke belief, vision, and urgency
Tactical Argument Dismantle opposing views swiftly

Glossary

Term Definition
Ethos Appeal to credibility or character
Logos Appeal to logic and reason
Pathos Appeal to emotion
Counterargument A viewpoint that opposes your main argument
Diction The specific word choice used to influence tone and meaning
Delivery The vocal and physical performance of a speech
Rhetorical Question A question posed for effect, not meant to be answered
Kairos The opportune moment to speak or act rhetorically
